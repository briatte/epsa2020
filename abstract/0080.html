Does Message Distortion by Rivals Affect Party Policy Images? Observational and Experimental Evidence
Zeynep Somer-Topcu 1, Margit Tavits 2
1 University of Texas at Austin, Austin
2 Washington University, St. Louis, St. Louis

How do rival parties’ characterization of each other’s policy positions affect voter perceptions of these positions? To our knowledge, this question has not received any attention in the comparative party politics literature. Despite the increasing scholarly interest in voter perception of party positions, prior work has focused on parties’ own messages only, ignoring the fact that such messages never occur in a vacuum. Parties are engaged in constant exchange with their rivals about their policy positions. This creates possibilities for rival parties to misconstrue each other’s policy messages. We argue that rival party distortion of party messages bias voters’ perceptions of party positions. This effect should be particularly strong for those respondents who identify with the rival party given the main argument of the partisan motivated reasoning literature that partisans pay more attention to their own party messages. Using observational data across nine European democracies and experimental evidence from the UK, we show support for our hypotheses. Our results have important implications for our understanding of party campaign strategies and their voter-level consequences.

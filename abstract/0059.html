P2: Comparative Politics
Panel Session 2
Room: Zoom
Moderator/s:
Anselm Hager
Feminist Backlash Against Political Islam: Evidence from an Experiment in Germany
Mathias Poertner 1, Donghyun Danny Choi 2, Nicholas Sambanis 3
1 Texas A&M University, College Station
2 University of Pittsburgh, Pittsburgh
3 University of Pennsylvania, Philadelphia

To what extent is discrimination against Muslim immigrants in Western societies driven by perceptions of a cultural threat posed by political Islam? We hypothesize that natives' attitudes toward Muslim immigrants are based partly on the belief that Muslims hold conservative attitudes towards women and their role in society. Such beliefs will shape anti-immigrant bias among native women in particular---an effect that we describe as the "feminist backlash hypothesis.'' We test this hypothesis in a large-scale field experiment in 25 cities across three German states, during which 3,800 unknowing bystanders were exposed to a brief social encounter with a confederate who revealed her ideas regarding gender roles. The confederate is always female and we vary her religious and ethnic identity. We find significant bias against Muslim confederates, but the bias is eliminated when bystanders are exposed to information suggesting that the Muslim confederate holds progressive attitudes toward women. This effect is observed only among female bystanders. Our results highlight the centrality of ideational stereotypes in structuring opposition to immigration.
